\begin{abstract}
Auxiliary tasks for representation learning are widely advocated for reinforcement learning in
visually rich, chaotic-physics environments, yet most evaluations rely on benchmark simulators
where the action--reward link is by construction non-degenerate. We study three auxiliary
configurations---no auxiliary task (baseline), object-centric pose prediction (OCA), and
next-frame pixel reconstruction (DPR)---in a real commercial mobile basketball game
(Bouncy Basketball) running on Android emulators. We find that the environment's autopilot
opponent and player-team auto-control jointly create a near-degenerate reward landscape in
which uniform-random policies are locally near-optimal; consequently, PPO with auxiliary tasks
alone fails to commit to a non-trivial policy. Adding Random Network Distillation (RND) as an
intrinsic reward stream restores PPO commitment and yields measurable gains, but the
relative ordering of the three auxiliary configurations differs between training-time (rolling
returns) and deterministic evaluation. We additionally contribute (i) a lightweight CV-pipeline
object-centric target requiring zero supervision, and (ii) a diagnostic protocol
(\texttt{diag\_actions}) for detecting degenerate reward landscapes before training.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% TODO 3-4 paragraphs:
% Paragraph 1: motivation -- chaotic physics RL is hard, aux tasks help in benchmarks

% Paragraph 4: contributions of this paper

Model-free deep reinforcement learning struggles with chaotic, physics-based environments
where small differences in initial state or action timing produce qualitatively different
outcomes \citep{schulman2017ppo, jaderberg2017unreal}. Auxiliary tasks for
representation learning---predicting object positions, next-frame pixels, or self-supervised
contrastive targets---have been proposed as a remedy, training a shared encoder to extract
features useful for both the auxiliary objective and downstream policy learning
\citep{laskin2020curl,lyle2022what}.

Most evaluations of auxiliary tasks rely on benchmark simulators (Atari, DeepMind Lab, Procgen,
MuJoCo) where the action--reward link is by construction non-degenerate: every action
meaningfully shifts state, and PPO has a well-defined gradient to follow. Real commercial games
deployed on consumer platforms introduce challenges these benchmarks elide. The action space
exposed by the game's UI may be impoverished relative to its internal mechanics
(\textit{e.g.}, a single tap that triggers context-dependent behavior). The opponent
controller may be hardcoded to a passive defensive role that effectively eliminates the
reward signal in one direction. Snapshot-based episodic reset, vision-based pose extraction,
and emulator failure handling all become first-class engineering concerns rather than
afterthoughts.

We study three auxiliary configurations---no auxiliary task (baseline), object-centric
pose prediction (OCA), and next-frame pixel reconstruction (DPR)---in a real commercial
mobile basketball game (Bouncy Basketball) running on a farm of Android emulators. Our
central empirical finding is that the environment's auto-control dynamics jointly create a
\emph{near-degenerate reward landscape}, in which uniform-random policies are locally
near-optimal and PPO with auxiliary tasks alone fails to commit. Augmenting PPO with
Random Network Distillation \citep{burda2018rnd} restores commitment, and at this point the
three auxiliary configurations differentiate.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item An empirical comparison of OCA, DPR, and baseline auxiliary configurations on a real
    commercial Android game, under both pure PPO and PPO with RND.
  \item A diagnostic methodology (\texttt{diag\_actions}) for detecting degenerate
    action--reward landscapes \emph{before} committing to a training run.
  \item A lightweight CV-pipeline object-centric target via HSV thresholding and connected
    components, requiring zero human-annotated supervision.
  \item Documentation of the engineering pitfalls of deploying RL on a closed-source
    commercial mobile game (Sec.~\ref{sec:env}), including team-mapping verification,
    snapshot portability, and autopilot characterization.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Auxiliary tasks for RL.}
UNREAL \citep{jaderberg2017unreal} introduced multi-task auxiliary losses (pixel control,
reward prediction, value replay) shared with a base actor-critic, finding that auxiliary
prediction of \emph{changes} outperforms raw pixel reconstruction. CURL \citep{laskin2020curl}
replaces reconstruction with contrastive instance-discrimination and outperforms
reconstruction-based baselines on Atari and DM-Control. \citet{lyle2022what} investigate
which auxiliary tasks are useful in theory and find the answer is sensitive to the target
policy and the environment's reward density. Our work adds an empirical data point to this
open question, on a real-world game with explicit autopilot dynamics.

\paragraph{Object-centric world models.}
Recent work \citep{ferraro2024focus, mosbach2025ocstorm} uses learned segmentation networks
(slot attention, or pre-trained masks) to extract per-object representations, then trains
world models that predict per-object dynamics. These methods report sample-efficiency gains
on robotic manipulation tasks but require either pre-trained vision models or
human-annotated bounding boxes. We use a simpler alternative: HSV thresholding plus
connected components, which extracts object centroids in ${\sim}1$\,ms per frame with zero
supervision, at the cost of some detection noise.

\paragraph{Intrinsic motivation.}
Random Network Distillation \citep{burda2018rnd} computes intrinsic reward as the prediction
error between a fixed random network and a learned predictor on the current observation,
giving high reward for visiting novel states. ICM \citep{pathak2017curiosity} uses a
forward-dynamics model instead. We adopt RND for its simplicity and demonstrate its
necessity in our degenerate-reward setting.

\paragraph{Mobile / Android RL.}
AndroidEnv \citep{toyama2021androidenv} is the closest prior work: a generic RL platform for
any Android app exposed through a universal touchscreen interface. AndroidEnv targets
infrastructure (an open Android stack for RL research) rather than any specific game. We
target a specific commercial game and document the engineering pitfalls that arise when
auxiliary tasks meet such a setting.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

We train Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) \citep{schulman2017ppo} with a shared encoder
that feeds three heads: an actor (policy logits), a critic (value), and an auxiliary head
whose form depends on the configuration. Optionally we augment PPO with an intrinsic reward
stream from Random Network Distillation \citep{burda2018rnd}.

\subsection{Architecture}

The encoder is a NatureCNN \citep{mnih2015nature} taking a 4-frame stack of $84\times 84$
grayscale observations and producing a 512-dimensional feature vector $z_t$. The actor head
is a linear projection to action logits; the critic is a linear projection to scalar value.

\subsection{Auxiliary configurations}

\paragraph{Baseline.} No auxiliary head; the encoder is shaped only by PPO gradients.

\paragraph{OCA (Object-Centric Auxiliary).} A 2-layer MLP head predicts a 10-dimensional
target $\hat{y}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{10}$ representing ball $(x,y)$, our team centroid
$(x,y,\sin\theta,\cos\theta)$, and opponent centroid $(x,y,\sin\theta,\cos\theta)$, where
$\theta$ is the body axis derived from the line between same-team players. The target is
shifted by $K=4$ steps into the future to prevent the encoder from trivially solving
``detect objects in the current frame.'' Loss is masked mean squared error against the CV
pipeline output (Sec.~\ref{sec:env}).

\paragraph{DPR (Dense Pixel Reconstruction).} A transposed-convolutional decoder predicts
the next-frame's grayscale at $84\times 84$. Loss is mean squared error against the actual
next frame from the frame stack.

\subsection{Random Network Distillation}

% TODO formula + normalization details

Two networks share a small-CNN architecture: $f_\theta^\text{tgt}$ (random, frozen at
initialization) and $f_\phi^\text{prd}$ (trained). The intrinsic reward is
\begin{equation}
  r^\text{int}_t = \beta \cdot \frac{\|f_\theta^\text{tgt}(o_t) - f_\phi^\text{prd}(o_t)\|_2^2}{\sqrt{\sigma^2_\text{run} + \epsilon}}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma^2_\text{run}$ is a running estimate of the squared-norm variance and
$\beta=0.5$. The predictor is trained on a separate MSE loss to match the target's outputs
on visited states.

\subsection{Combined objective}

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_\text{pg} + c_v \mathcal{L}_v - c_\text{ent} \mathcal{H}[\pi] + c_\text{aux} \mathcal{L}_\text{aux} + c_\text{rnd} \mathcal{L}_\text{rnd}
\end{equation}
with $c_v=0.5$, $c_\text{ent}=0.05$, $c_\text{aux}=0.5$, $c_\text{rnd}=1.0$.
