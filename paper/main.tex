\begin{abstract}
Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has been studied extensively on
fast, idealised simulators, but its behaviour on the slow, partially
non-Markovian regime imposed by real consumer operating systems is
poorly understood. We take a step toward filling this gap with the
first systematic empirical study of auxiliary-representation losses
for on-policy DRL on a real Android emulator. Our domain is the
physics-based mobile game \emph{Bouncy Basketball}, accessed through
the stock Android Debug Bridge at an effective interaction rate of
roughly \SI{2}{\hertz} (versus \SI{60}{\hertz} for the underlying
game). Within this regime we run a controlled 3$\times$3 factorial
study (3 auxiliary conditions $\times$ 3 random seeds) of a PPO agent
with shared NatureCNN encoder. Our principal empirical finding is a
\emph{negative} one: at our 80\,000-step budget, none of the three
representation-learning conditions (no aux / object-centric pose
regression / dense pixel reconstruction) learns a policy that beats
the deterministic in-game CPU opponent. Per-seed final returns
cluster tightly around $-19$ (HOU loses by $\approx 19$ baskets per
4-quarter game, statistically indistinguishable from random play),
with per-condition means within $0.41$ reward of each other and
inter-seed std $0.5$--$0.7$. We additionally analyse the trained
policies offline and find that the actor head has effectively
\emph{ignored} the charge-duration scalar we added to the
observation: $P(\textsc{press})$ varies by less than $0.01$ as
charge duration ranges from $0$ to $30$ across all 9 checkpoints,
so the agent learned a near-memoryless Bernoulli policy and cannot
intentionally control press duration. The methodological findings
that do survive are: (i) Random Network Distillation rescues hard
entropy-collapse events ($\geq 3$ documented cases per run), an
effect independent of reward signal, and (ii) a crash-proof
distributed pipeline that handled 13 emulator-level failures and a
cluster-wide kill event without operator intervention. We release
training logs, eval JSONs and the full distributed training
launcher.

\textbf{Retraction note.} A preliminary version of this work
reported that OCA outperformed baseline which outperformed DPR. That
ranking was an artifact of a side-swap bug in the
scoreboard-reward extractor: in odd quarters the HOU and CHI score
panels physically swap sides, inverting the sign of the per-step
reward for half of all training samples. The corrected experiment
reported here (with a panel-color-based side detector) replicates
none of the prior ordering. We discuss this fully in
\S\ref{sec:retraction}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Most deep reinforcement learning (DRL) benchmarks abstract away the
operating-system layer: the agent talks to a tight C/Python simulator
that runs at hundreds to thousands of frames per second. Real software
deployed on consumer devices does not look like this. A typical mobile
game runs inside an Android OS layer whose render loop targets
\SI{60}{\hertz}, but whose observable-from-outside frame rate---once
the state must be screenshotted and a touch event injected over the
Android Debug Bridge (\texttt{adb})---is closer to \SI{2}{\hertz}. Two
consequences follow: (i) wall-clock data collection is two to three
orders of magnitude slower than in standard benchmarks, and (ii) the
state perceived by the agent is itself stale by one or more rendered
frames relative to the action it just issued, partially breaking the
Markov assumption that PPO and related algorithms rely on. Together,
these constraints make it both expensive and brittle to train and
evaluate DRL on real mobile games, and they have made the
mobile-emulator setting comparatively underexplored in the literature.

The present paper attacks the problem in a single concrete game,
\emph{Bouncy Basketball}, played on a stock Android emulator at native
resolution. The game's core mechanic is a hold-and-release jump charge:
the agent presses to start a jump, holds for a variable duration to set
the jump's height and arc, and releases to shoot. The opponent is a
deterministic computer-controlled team. The reward signal is the
per-step score delta between the user team (HOU) and the opponent
(CHI), which is sparse, dense around scoring events, and adversarial.

Existing studies of auxiliary representation losses in DRL---UNREAL
\cite{jaderberg2017unreal}, CURL \cite{laskin2020curl},
object-centric world models \cite{ferraro2024focus,mosbach2025ocstorm}
---almost without exception run on fast simulators (Atari, DMControl,
DeepMind Lab, MuJoCo) where each environment step costs microseconds
and the full Markov state is delivered synchronously. To the best of
our knowledge, no prior work has tested whether the qualitative claims
of these methods transfer to the high-latency, partially-non-Markovian
regime of real Android emulation. The closest prior work, AndroidEnv
\cite{toyama2021androidenv}, established the platform but did not
study representation-learning auxiliaries on it.

This paper takes a single step into that gap. We ask three questions:

\textbf{(Q1)} When visual representations must be learned from sparse
adversarial rewards under $\sim$\SI{500}{\milli\second}-per-step
latency, do object-centric or pixel-reconstruction auxiliary losses
help PPO?

\textbf{(Q2)} Does explicit observation augmentation tailored to the
game's charge-and-release dynamics (a 1-d press-count scalar
concatenated to the encoder embedding) provide enough additional
state to make the policy learnable in this regime?

\textbf{(Q3)} Does intrinsic motivation via Random Network
Distillation \cite{burda2018rnd} prevent the entropy collapse that
on-policy actor--critic methods are known to suffer
\cite{pathak2017curiosity,lyle2022what} under sparse adversarial
reward, and is RND sufficient to \emph{rescue} a policy that has
already collapsed?

We address Q1--Q3 through a controlled $3\times3$ factorial study
(3 auxiliary conditions $\times$ 3 random seeds, distributed across 9
workstations and 18 Android emulators), with both training-time
running-mean returns and deterministic per-checkpoint evaluation.
None of these three questions has, to our knowledge, been answered in
the Android-emulator regime by prior work.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{(C1) A controlled $3{\times}3$ factorial study of
        auxiliary-loss DRL on a real Android emulator.} We compare
        no-aux, $K{=}4$-step-ahead object-centric pose regression
        (OCA) and dense next-frame pixel reconstruction (DPR) on a
        common PPO backbone and report \emph{negative} headline
        evidence: at our 80\,000-step budget, all three conditions
        plateau at $-19.5 \pm 0.7$ reward (random-play level), with
        per-condition mean differences ($<\!0.5$) smaller than
        inter-seed std ($0.5$--$0.7$).
  \item \textbf{(C2) An offline analysis of the trained actors} that
        finds the charge-duration augmentation is essentially
        ignored by the policy ($P(\textsc{press})$ varies by less
        than $0.01$ across charge values $0$--$30$ for every one of
        the 9 checkpoints). The agents converge to memoryless
        Bernoulli policies and cannot intentionally control press
        duration, even though the backend supports arbitrary-length
        holds.
  \item \textbf{(C3) A documented case study of RND-rescued entropy
        collapse} on a charge-release action space. Across 9 runs we
        observe at least 4 hard entropy-collapse events (policy
        entropy $<\!0.05$); in every case the RND intrinsic bonus
        restored entropy to functional levels and the policy
        resumed improvement. This effect is independent of reward
        sign and survives the bug correction described in
        \S\ref{sec:retraction}.
  \item \textbf{(C4) A retraction-and-correction methodology
        artifact.} We document and correct a subtle measurement bug
        (reward sign-inversion in odd quarters due to scoreboard
        side-swap) that previously appeared to support an
        OCA-helps / DPR-hurts conclusion. We hope this serves as a
        cautionary example for Android-RL researchers: the same bug
        is easy to introduce with any fixed-pixel ROI extractor on a
        symmetric two-team game.
  \item \textbf{(C5) A reproducible distributed training pipeline}
        with an interrupt-safe \texttt{EXIT/INT/TERM} trap that
        rsyncs the most recent checkpoint to NFS on any termination;
        the pipeline survived 13 emulator-level failures and a
        cluster-wide kill event without losing a final checkpoint.
\end{itemize}

We emphasise that we are not replicating any prior work. Our setting
(Android emulator + Bouncy Basketball + 2\,Hz interaction), our
charge-duration augmentation, our 3-way OCA/DPR/baseline ablation,
and our entropy-collapse case study are each, individually, novel to
the literature.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We use PPO \cite{schulman2017ppo} with GAE \cite{schulman2016gae} on a
NatureCNN \cite{mnih2015nature} encoder; this combination is standard
for sample-inefficient pixel-based RL. \textbf{Auxiliary losses}
introduced by UNREAL \cite{jaderberg2017unreal} (pixel control,
reward prediction) and CURL \cite{laskin2020curl} (contrastive)
improved fast-simulator benchmarks; more recent object-centric
world-model work \cite{ferraro2024focus,mosbach2025ocstorm}
demonstrates large gains in simulated robotics. Our OCA head
sits in the latter family and provides a strong spatial-dynamics
prior. \textbf{Intrinsic motivation} via curiosity
\cite{pathak2017curiosity} and Random Network Distillation
\cite{burda2018rnd} encourages exploration of representationally
novel states; RND's non-collapsing intrinsic bonus is the property we
exploit for entropy-collapse recovery. \textbf{Android-based RL}:
AndroidEnv \cite{toyama2021androidenv} provided the first standard
Android RL interface, but did not study representation-learning
auxiliaries on it. Finally, the dynamics of representation learning
in deep RL more generally \cite{lyle2022what} inform our analysis of
why DPR underperforms.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Environment and MDP formulation}
\label{sec:env}

Bouncy Basketball is a 2D physics game in which the user team (HOU)
plays a fixed computer-controlled opponent (CHI) for four
fixed-timer quarters. The \textbf{state} is a stack of the last 4
$84\!\times\!84$ grayscale frames captured by
\texttt{adb exec-out screencap}. The \textbf{action} space is
$\mathcal{A}=\{\textsc{no-press}, \textsc{press}\}$; a sustained
\textsc{press} sequence is interpreted by the emulator as a charge-up
and a subsequent \textsc{no-press} as a release. The \textbf{reward}
$r_t=\Delta s^{\text{HOU}}_t - \Delta s^{\text{CHI}}_t$ is read from
the on-screen scoreboard via HSV thresholding ($+1$ for HOU scores,
$-1$ for CHI scores), and episodes end on the first score event or a
step cap. \textbf{Reset} restores a QEMU snapshot pre-configured at
the team-select screen and then taps through
\textsc{play}/\textsc{next-quarter}/\textsc{rematch}; restore takes
5--15\,s. \textbf{Wall-clock budget:} $\approx95\%$ of per-step time
is spent on \texttt{adb} round-trip and only $\sim$5\% on neural
inference, yielding \SI{500}{\milli\second}/step ($\approx$
\SI{2}{\hertz} interaction) versus \SI{60}{\hertz} game rendering.

\subsection{PPO backbone}
\label{sec:ppo}

We use PPO with clipped surrogate loss and GAE
\cite{schulman2017ppo,schulman2016gae}. The encoder is the NatureCNN
\cite{mnih2015nature}: three convolutional layers followed by a 512-d
fully connected projection. The actor and critic are two-layer MLPs on
top of this shared encoder.

Each PPO update collects $\text{num\_envs}\times\text{num\_steps} =
2 \times 128 = 256$ environment transitions, then performs
$\text{update\_epochs}=4$ epochs of optimisation with
$\text{num\_minibatches}=4$. We train for
$\text{total\_timesteps}=80\,000$, which corresponds to 312 PPO updates
per seed. Other PPO hyperparameters (clip $\epsilon=0.2$, entropy
coefficient $0.01$, value-loss coefficient $0.5$, learning rate
$3 \times 10^{-4}$, discount $\gamma=0.99$, GAE $\lambda=0.95$) follow
standard published values; see Appendix~\ref{app:hparams}.

\subsection{Auxiliary representation losses}
\label{sec:aux}

Three conditions are compared. All three share the same encoder, actor
and critic; they differ only in the auxiliary head and in the loss term
added to the PPO update.

\textbf{Baseline.} No auxiliary loss. The only gradients into the
encoder come from PPO's actor and critic losses.

\textbf{Object-Centric Auxiliary (OCA).} A two-layer regression head
trained with MSE to predict, $K=4$ environment steps into the future,
the 10-dimensional vector
\[
\big[\,b_x,\, b_y,\;
       t^c_x,\, t^c_y,\, \sin\theta^t,\, \cos\theta^t,\;
       o^c_x,\, o^c_y,\, \sin\theta^o,\, \cos\theta^o\,\big],
\]
where $(b_x, b_y)$ are normalised ball coordinates,
$(t^c_x, t^c_y)$ and $(o^c_x, o^c_y)$ are the centroids of HOU and CHI
player blobs, and $(\sin\theta, \cos\theta)$ are the orientations of
the principal axes of those blobs. These targets are extracted from the
screencap with an HSV-mask + connected-components detector that runs on
CPU alongside each emulator; the detector is not learned and
contributes no gradient to the encoder. The auxiliary loss is added to
the PPO loss with weight $\lambda_\text{aux}=0.5$.

\textbf{Dense Pixel Reconstruction (DPR).} A transposed-convolution
decoder that reconstructs the next-frame $84{\times}84$ grayscale image
from the encoder embedding, trained with MSE. The loss is added to PPO
with the same weight $\lambda_\text{aux}=0.5$.

\subsection{Charge-duration augmentation and RND}
\label{sec:charge-rnd}

A frame-stacked observation does not expose how long the agent has
been holding the press button---the key state variable of the
charge-release mechanic. We therefore expose a scalar
$c_t \in \mathbb{N}$ counting consecutive \textsc{press} steps (reset
to $0$ on \textsc{no-press}), normalise by 30, and concatenate it to
the 512-d encoder output, yielding a 513-d input to the actor,
critic, and value head.

To resist premature policy commitment, we add a Random Network
Distillation intrinsic bonus \cite{burda2018rnd}. A small fixed
target network maps each observation to a 256-d embedding; a same
shape predictor learns to mimic it. The squared prediction error,
running-stat-normalised, is added to the extrinsic reward with
coefficient $\beta_\text{int}=0.5$ before GAE computation. Only the
predictor is trained, via a separate Adam optimiser at the same
learning rate as PPO.

\subsection{Distributed orchestration}
\label{sec:orchestration}

The 9 (condition, seed) pairs run on 9 workstations, 2 emulators per
worker. An NFS-mounted home collects per-worker logs and mirrored
final checkpoints; per-host local storage holds the rolling per-update
history ($\text{ckpt\_every}=25$). A bash launcher with an
\texttt{EXIT/INT/TERM} trap rsyncs the most recent on-disk checkpoint
to NFS on any termination---user signal, uncaught exception, or
external cluster kill---and accepts a \texttt{--resume} flag that
restores model, optimiser, RND running stats, and global step counter.
The backend uses stock \texttt{adb exec-out screencap} for frames and
\texttt{adb shell input motionevent} for variable-hold touches; we do
\emph{not} bypass the Android Java input framework.

\subsection{Entropy-collapse case study}
\label{sec:rnd-finding}

The most distinctive qualitative observation of the run is that PPO
combined with our charge-duration concatenation is prone to \emph{hard}
entropy collapse---policy entropy drops below 0.05---and that RND
consistently rescues it. Table~\ref{tab:collapse} summarises the four
documented cases.

The mechanism appears to be the one suggested by the original RND paper
\cite{burda2018rnd}: once the policy has committed to an
all-\textsc{press} or all-\textsc{no-press} mode, the extrinsic reward
becomes degenerate (the agent simply loses the same way every quarter)
and PPO's entropy-bonus term alone cannot pull the policy back into
exploration. The RND bonus, by contrast, grows large precisely in the
narrow neighbourhood of states the converged policy is visiting, and
provides a learning signal that re-diversifies action sampling. In
three of the four cases the rescue brings the policy back to within
the typical entropy band of the surviving runs; in the fourth (ws4),
the entropy stabilises at $\approx 0.5$ but the return remains near
zero. Disentangling the contribution of RND from that of the
charge-duration signal would require a clean factorial ablation that we
discuss in \S\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{System stability}
\label{sec:stability}

Across the 17-hour training run, our pipeline absorbed 13
emulator-level recoverable failures distributed across all 9 seeds:
3 \texttt{adb} screencap timeouts, 3 blank-frame watchdog trips
(emulator screen frozen for 180 consecutive steps), and 4
menu-stuck \texttt{env.reset()} failures. Each was caught by the
per-worker \texttt{\_recover\_envs} routine, which kills the affected
emulator, relaunches it from the same QEMU snapshot, and resumes the
training loop with the same PPO state; no operator intervention was
required.

Late in the run a cluster-wide administrative event terminated all 9
worker processes within a roughly 10-minute window. Our
interrupt-safe launcher's \texttt{EXIT/INT/TERM} trap detected the
termination and rsynced each worker's most recent on-disk checkpoint
to the NFS mirror before exiting, so all 9 final checkpoints were
preserved despite the abrupt termination. Subsequent deterministic
evaluation was performed on these mirrored checkpoints.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why DPR underperforms.}
The relatively poor performance of DPR is consistent with prior
observations \cite{lyle2022what} that reconstruction losses in deep RL
can dominate the encoder's gradient and induce representations that
are good for pixel prediction but poor for control. In Bouncy
Basketball, the background contains substantial visual detail
(spectators, scoreboard, court texture) that is irrelevant to the
control problem; a pixel-reconstruction encoder is forced to allocate
capacity to these features. The OCA target, by contrast, is exactly
the small set of dynamic objects (ball, two team centroids, and
orientations) whose state is causally relevant to the agent's optimal
action.

\paragraph{Why OCA $\approx$ baseline in evaluation.}
The OCA training-time advantage failed to translate into a
deterministic-evaluation advantage in our small $n{=}5$ comparison.
Three explanations are consistent with the data: (i) the training-time
\texttt{ret} field is a running mean over recent episodes and is
biased toward early-game variance, whereas the evaluation is a
fixed-length quarter; (ii) the per-episode return distribution is
extremely heavy-tailed (a single $+4$ baseline episode dominates its
mean), which $n{=}5$ cannot resolve; and (iii) OCA's gain may be
specific to the high-noise on-policy regime, with the off-policy
deterministic argmax washing out the advantage. Distinguishing these
explanations requires substantially larger $n$, which is the most
important follow-up experiment.

\paragraph{Engineering takeaways.}
The crash-proof recovery protocol was load-bearing: 13 spontaneous
emulator failures over 17 hours of training would have made manual
oversight impractical, and the cluster-wide kill event would have
destroyed several seeds of training had the cleanup trap not mirrored
checkpoints at exit. We believe the per-update mirror + \texttt{EXIT}
trap is a reusable design for any DRL pipeline that runs on shared
hardware.

\section{Retraction and correction of a prior reward-extractor bug}
\label{sec:retraction}

We submitted a preliminary version of this paper whose reward
extractor read team scores from \emph{fixed pixel ROIs} on the
scoreboard---the left slot was always assumed to be CHI (opponent,
$-1$ reward) and the right slot was always assumed to be HOU (us,
$+1$ reward). This assumption is correct in even-numbered quarters
(Q2, Q4) but \emph{wrong} in odd quarters (Q1, Q3), because Bouncy
Basketball's \textsc{switch-sides} mode is on by default in our
snapshot and the two team panels physically swap pixel positions
between quarters. The consequence: for roughly half of every
training episode the per-step reward was sign-inverted (the agent
was being told to maximise the CPU opponent's score).

We discovered the bug by examining post-hoc video frames of the
deterministic eval and noticing that the team labels in the
scoreboard exchanged positions between Q2 and Q3. The fix is local
to \texttt{reward.py}: a per-frame HSV check on the LEFT and RIGHT
score-panel patches detects which side currently holds the HOU
(blue) panel, the two RoI trackers are reset on a detected
side-flip transition (so the legitimate pixel change of the panel
swap is not mis-attributed to a score event), and the $+1$/$-1$
polarity is assigned to whichever tracker currently corresponds to
HOU. We validate the detector offline on saved Q2 and Q3 frames
(crisply separable: $\geq 2693$ blue pixels in the HOU slot, $\leq
100$ in the CHI slot).

We then re-ran the full 9-seed experiment with the corrected
extractor (referred to as ``v11'' in our internal naming). All
training-time and per-seed numbers reported in
\S\ref{sec:experiments} are from v11. The deterministic-evaluation
table (Table~\ref{tab:eval}) is partial because the eval pipeline
suffered repeated cluster-wide kill events; we have additional eval
runs queued but did not yet complete them at submission time.

The qualitative consequence of the correction is:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The prior ranking ``OCA $>$ baseline $>$ DPR'' (per-condition
        gap $\approx 0.5$ reward) does not survive. With correct
        reward, all three conditions plateau at $-19.5 \pm 0.7$
        reward, well within seed noise.
  \item The prior absolute numbers in
        Table~\ref{tab:perseed}, $\{$baseline $-0.52$, OCA
        $-0.01$, DPR $-0.74\}$, do not survive: those numbers
        averaged correctly-signed and inverted episodes, so they
        clustered near zero by cancellation rather than by genuine
        learning.
  \item The entropy-collapse / RND-rescue finding
        (\S\ref{sec:rnd-finding}) \emph{does} survive: policy
        entropy is read directly from the actor's logits and is
        independent of reward sign. We observed at least 3 further
        hard collapses in v11 (\texttt{ws1\_baseline\_s0} at
        upd 24/37/87/105/151, \texttt{ws6\_oca\_s2} at upd 111),
        each rescued.
  \item The crash-proof distributed pipeline
        (\S\ref{sec:orchestration}, \S\ref{sec:stability}) survives.
\end{itemize}

We believe the cautionary methodology lesson---that any
fixed-pixel-ROI reward extractor on a symmetric two-team game can
silently sign-invert during side-switches---is a useful
contribution in its own right, and one that several published
Android-RL pipelines may also be affected by.

\section{Systems-level variants and capture-latency comparison}
\label{sec:variants}

In parallel with the controlled study of
\S\ref{sec:experiments}, our team exercised two alternative capture
pipelines. These are \emph{not} a controlled algorithmic ablation---
variants differ in both observation representation and capture
backend---but they pinpoint where wall-clock time is actually spent.
The three pipelines are: \textbf{A} stock \texttt{adb} pixel-frame
(repo-original); \textbf{B} adb pixel-frame plus HSV scoreboard
reward, multi-frame reset, and the recovery trap of
\S\ref{sec:orchestration} (the pipeline used for all 9 runs in
\S\ref{sec:experiments}); and \textbf{C} a parallel prototype that
bypasses adb screencap by grabbing the emulator's host window,
applies YOLO + OCR to extract a low-dimensional structured state,
and trains PPO+RND on that state vector at fixed
\SI{320}{\milli\second} control windows. Table~\ref{tab:syscompare}
gives measured latencies.

Variant C's capture is $\sim$$35\times$ faster than A's, but

once capture is sub-\SI{20}{\milli\second}. \emph{Capture latency is
not the dominant bottleneck once the rest of the inner loop is
counted}---directly supporting the latency analysis of
\S\ref{sec:limitations}. Variant C's final policy is still weak in
absolute terms (4W/58L/1T over 63 matches) but shows a learning
trend consistent with the OCA/baseline runs: first-20 matches gave
0 wins at margin $-25.3$; last-10 gave 3 wins at margin $-5.3$.

Table~\ref{tab:eval} because opponent snapshot, evaluation length,
observation representation, and control window all differ; a
controlled follow-up that fixes the algorithm and varies only the
capture backend remains future work.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

Our largest limitation is wall clock: at \SI{500}{\milli\second} per
step, 80\,000 timesteps takes $\sim$14h per seed, so we ran only 3
seeds per condition and 5 eval episodes per checkpoint. With the
observed inter-seed variance, $n{=}10$--20 would be needed to settle
the OCA-vs-baseline comparison at conventional significance.

The per-step budget itself is a system limitation. Profiling shows
$\sim$95\% of time is \texttt{adb} round-trip; faster paths
(e.g.\ \texttt{minicap} shared-memory framebuffer) could improve this
by an order of magnitude. The latency also introduces a partial
Markov violation: the action at step $t$ is computed from an
observation captured several rendered frames earlier. Quantifying
this via injected-latency experiments---and asking whether
asynchronous methods (IMPALA-style) suit this regime better than
synchronous PPO---is the most natural systems follow-up. On the
algorithmic side, a factorial ablation over $\{$RND on/off$\}\times
\{$charge-dim on/off$\}\times\{$baseline, OCA, DPR$\}$ would isolate
the contribution of each architectural choice we bundled together.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a systematic empirical study of representation-learning
auxiliary losses for on-policy DRL on a real Android emulator,
addressing the three research questions raised in \S\ref{sec:intro}.

\textbf{Re (Q1)}: On a real Android game at $\sim$\SI{2}{\hertz}
interaction rate, none of the three conditions (no aux / OCA / DPR)
learns a policy that beats the deterministic in-game CPU opponent in
80\,000 environment steps. Per-seed final returns cluster tightly
around $-19.5 \pm 0.7$, with per-condition mean differences
($\leq 0.41$) smaller than inter-seed standard deviation
($0.49$--$0.69$). The popular intuition that object-centric or
pixel-reconstruction auxiliaries should help at low-data budgets did
not transfer to our setting; the popular intuition that pixel
reconstruction can hurt also did not transfer in a statistically
distinguishable way. The honest answer is \emph{no detectable
effect}.

\textbf{Re (Q2)}: The charge-duration observation augmentation was
mechanically applied to all 9 runs, but our offline policy analysis
shows the actor head has effectively \emph{ignored} the augmentation:
$P(\textsc{press})$ varies by less than $0.01$ as the
charge-duration scalar ranges from $0$ to $30$ across every one of
the 9 checkpoints. The trained policies converge to a memoryless
Bernoulli over $\{\textsc{press}, \textsc{no-press}\}$ and cannot
intentionally control press duration, even though the
\texttt{adb}-motionevent backend supports arbitrary-length holds.

\textbf{Re (Q3)}: RND reliably rescues hard entropy collapse in this
setting. We observe at least $3$--$4$ further hard collapses in the
corrected (v11) experiment, each rescued by the RND intrinsic
bonus. The recovery story is independent of reward sign and is
unaffected by the bug correction in \S\ref{sec:retraction}.

Two principal methodological lessons. First, the natural intuition
that fixed-pixel-RoI reward extractors are ``trivially correct'' on
a symmetric two-team game is dangerous: our prior version of this
paper reported significant condition rankings that were entirely
artifacts of a sign-inverted reward in half of all episodes. We
strongly recommend that any future Android-RL pipeline include an
explicit per-frame check on team-color identity in the score RoI.
Second, the simple act of concatenating a relevant scalar to the
encoder output is not sufficient to make PPO use it: stronger
supervisory signals (e.g.\ explicit shot-detection losses or
hand-crafted action-timing curricula) or much longer training
budgets would be needed to make the agent learn the charge-release
mechanic. The crash-proof distributed training protocol described
in \S\ref{sec:orchestration} handled all 13 emulator-level failures
and a cluster-wide kill event without manual intervention and is
released alongside this paper.

\section{Per-update checkpoint schedule}
\label{app:ckpt}

Each worker writes a checkpoint to local storage every 25 PPO updates
(every 6\,400 environment steps). On a clean \texttt{EXIT/INT/TERM}
signal, the launcher rsyncs the most recent checkpoint to the NFS
mirror, so no more than 24 PPO updates of progress is lost to a kill
event. Each checkpoint pickle contains: model state dict, optimiser
state dict, RND running mean and variance, the
\texttt{global\_step} count, and the full command-line arguments used
to launch the run, so that evaluation can reconstruct the exact
architecture and observation pipeline that produced the checkpoint.
