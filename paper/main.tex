\begin{abstract}
We study deep reinforcement learning on a real Android emulator running
the physics-based mobile game \emph{Bouncy Basketball}. The Android
pipeline imposes hard practical constraints: every environment step
requires an \texttt{adb} screencap and an input event, yielding a
wall-clock budget of roughly \SI{500}{\milli\second} per step and an
effective interaction rate of about \SI{2}{\hertz}. Within this
constrained regime we train a distributed Proximal Policy Optimization
(PPO) agent with a shared NatureCNN encoder and compare three
representation-learning conditions: \emph{baseline} (no auxiliary
loss), \emph{Object-Centric Auxiliary} (OCA, a $K{=}4$-step-ahead pose
regression head) and \emph{Dense Pixel Reconstruction} (DPR, a
transposed-convolution next-frame decoder). To prevent the policy from
prematurely committing in the high-noise reward landscape, we combine
PPO with Random Network Distillation (RND) intrinsic motivation and
expose a charge-duration scalar to the actor and critic to encode the
game's release-charge dynamics. Across 9 PPO runs (3 seeds $\times$ 3
conditions), training reward consistently ranks OCA above baseline above
DPR; deterministic evaluation at $n{=}5$ confirms that DPR is
significantly worst, while baseline and OCA are comparable within seed
noise. We additionally document four hard entropy-collapse events that
RND rescued, and present a crash-proof distributed training pipeline
that survived 13 emulator-level failures and a cluster-wide kill event
without operator intervention.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Most deep reinforcement learning (DRL) benchmarks abstract away the
operating-system layer: the agent talks to a tight C/Python simulator
that runs at hundreds to thousands of frames per second. Real software
deployed on consumer devices does not look like this. A typical mobile
game runs inside an Android OS layer whose render loop targets
\SI{60}{\hertz}, but whose observable-from-outside frame rate---once
the state must be screenshotted and a touch event injected over the
Android Debug Bridge (\texttt{adb})---is closer to \SI{2}{\hertz}. Two
consequences follow: (i) wall-clock data collection is two to three
orders of magnitude slower than in standard benchmarks, and (ii) the
state perceived by the agent is itself stale by one or more rendered
frames relative to the action it just issued, partially breaking the
Markov assumption that PPO and related algorithms rely on. Together,
these constraints make it both expensive and brittle to train and
evaluate DRL on real mobile games, and they have made the
mobile-emulator setting comparatively underexplored in the literature.

The present paper attacks the problem in a single concrete game,
\emph{Bouncy Basketball}, played on a stock Android emulator at native
resolution. The game's core mechanic is a hold-and-release jump charge:
the agent presses to start a jump, holds for a variable duration to set
the jump's height and arc, and releases to shoot. The opponent is a
deterministic computer-controlled team. The reward signal is the
per-step score delta between the user team (HOU) and the opponent
(CHI), which is sparse, dense around scoring events, and adversarial.

In this setting we ask a focused empirical question: \emph{when
visual representations must be learned end-to-end from sparse rewards
on slow data, do object-centric or pixel-reconstruction auxiliary
losses help PPO?} We compare two well-known auxiliary-task families
\cite{jaderberg2017unreal,laskin2020curl} against a no-aux baseline,
holding everything else fixed across three random seeds per condition.
We additionally integrate Random Network Distillation
\cite{burda2018rnd} as an intrinsic-motivation signal to break the
near-uniform-random equilibrium that the game's reward landscape
encourages \cite{pathak2017curiosity}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item An empirical study of three representation-learning conditions
        (no aux / OCA / DPR) on a real Android game at
        $\sim$\SI{2}{\hertz} interaction rate, with 9 PPO runs (3 seeds
        $\times$ 3 conditions) and both training-time and
        deterministic-evaluation metrics.
  \item Evidence that pixel reconstruction (DPR) consistently
        \emph{hurts} performance in this regime, while object-centric
        pose regression (OCA) modestly helps during training but only
        matches the baseline under deterministic evaluation.
  \item A documented case study of four hard entropy-collapse events
        during PPO training, each of which RND intrinsic motivation
        successfully rescued, allowing the policy to recover and
        continue improving.
  \item A crash-proof distributed pipeline that handled 13
        emulator-level failures (adb timeouts, blank-frame watchdogs,
        menu-stuck resets) and a cluster-wide kill event during
        training, with full checkpoint preservation via an
        interrupt-safe per-update protocol.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{PPO and on-policy RL.} We use Proximal Policy Optimization
\cite{schulman2017ppo} with Generalized Advantage Estimation
\cite{schulman2016gae} as our policy-gradient backbone. PPO has become
the default for sample-inefficient settings due to its trust-region
behaviour and small per-update wall cost; both properties are essential
here, because each environment step takes hundreds of milliseconds.

\paragraph{Auxiliary representation losses.} UNREAL
\cite{jaderberg2017unreal} introduced unsupervised auxiliary tasks
(pixel control, reward prediction) into the encoder of an actor--critic
network and reported gains on Atari and Labyrinth. CURL
\cite{laskin2020curl} applied contrastive self-supervision to RL from
pixels and improved sample efficiency on DMControl. More recent
object-centric world-model work \cite{ferraro2024focus,mosbach2025ocstorm}
shows that explicitly representing object identities and poses can
dramatically improve sample efficiency in simulated robotics. Our OCA
head sits in the same family: a $K{=}4$-step-ahead pose regression loss
that gives the encoder a strong prior toward spatial dynamics. The
dynamics of representation learning in deep RL more generally
\cite{lyle2022what,mnih2015nature} also inform our analysis.

\paragraph{Intrinsic motivation.} Curiosity-driven exploration
\cite{pathak2017curiosity} and Random Network Distillation
\cite{burda2018rnd} use prediction-error signals to encourage the
policy to visit states whose representations are novel to a randomly
initialised target network. We use RND specifically because its
intrinsic bonus does not collapse to zero even when the policy itself
has converged, which is precisely the recovery property we need in the
entropy-collapse cases that we observe.

\paragraph{Android-based RL.} AndroidEnv \cite{toyama2021androidenv}
provided the first standard RL interface to Android. We follow the same
high-level idea but run a custom Android Studio emulator on each
worker, with our own snapshot and reset protocol, because we need
deterministic per-episode initial conditions for the multi-quarter
game structure of Bouncy Basketball.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Environment and MDP formulation}
\label{sec:env}

\textbf{Game.} Bouncy Basketball is a 2D physics game in which the user
team (HOU) plays a fixed computer-controlled opponent (CHI) for four
quarters. Each quarter ends after a fixed timer; the score difference
across all four quarters determines win or loss.

\textbf{State.} We capture a single $84{\times}84$ grayscale frame per
environment step via \texttt{adb exec-out screencap}, then stack the
last four frames as the observation tensor.

\textbf{Action.} Following standard practice for charge-and-release
games, we discretise the user's input into two actions,
$\mathcal{A}=\{\textsc{no-press}, \textsc{press}\}$. The Android
emulator interprets a sustained \textsc{press} sequence as a charge-up
and a subsequent \textsc{no-press} as a release. We expose the running
press count to the agent as an auxiliary scalar (see
\S\ref{sec:charge}).

\textbf{Reward.} At each step we read the on-screen scoreboard via HSV
thresholding of a small ROI per team and recover the running scores
$s^{\text{HOU}}_t$ and $s^{\text{CHI}}_t$. The per-step reward is
\[
r_t \;=\; \Delta s^{\text{HOU}}_t \;-\; \Delta s^{\text{CHI}}_t,
\]
i.e. $+1$ when HOU scores and $-1$ when CHI scores, and $0$ otherwise.
Episodes terminate on the first score event (so successful baskets give
a positive terminal return) or after \texttt{max\_episode\_steps}
steps, whichever comes first. This keeps the reward signal dense
without resorting to hand-engineered shaping.

\textbf{Episode reset.} Resetting consists of (a) restoring the
emulator to a clean QEMU snapshot in which the team-select screen is
already configured (HOU vs CHI, no switch-sides, customised player),
and (b) tapping through \textsc{play} / \textsc{next-quarter} /
\textsc{rematch} buttons via the same backend the agent uses. Snapshot
restore takes 5--15\,s depending on host load, much faster than the
$\sim$30\,s cold boot but not the sub-second resets that standard
simulators enjoy. The reset routine has its own retry loop with a
hard-restart fallback if the menu is detected to be stuck after six
attempts.

\textbf{Wall-clock budget.} Profiling the inner loop, approximately
$95\%$ of per-step wall time is spent waiting on \texttt{adb}
(screencap and input round-trip), and only $\sim$$5\%$ on neural
inference; on our machines this works out to roughly
\SI{500}{\milli\second} per step, an effective interaction rate of
\SI{2}{\hertz}, even though the underlying game renders at
\SI{60}{\hertz}.

\subsection{PPO backbone}
\label{sec:ppo}

We use PPO with clipped surrogate loss and GAE
\cite{schulman2017ppo,schulman2016gae}. The encoder is the NatureCNN
\cite{mnih2015nature}: three convolutional layers followed by a 512-d
fully connected projection. The actor and critic are two-layer MLPs on
top of this shared encoder.

Each PPO update collects $\text{num\_envs}\times\text{num\_steps} =
2 \times 128 = 256$ environment transitions, then performs
$\text{update\_epochs}=4$ epochs of optimisation with
$\text{num\_minibatches}=4$. We train for
$\text{total\_timesteps}=80\,000$, which corresponds to 312 PPO updates
per seed. Other PPO hyperparameters (clip $\epsilon=0.2$, entropy
coefficient $0.01$, value-loss coefficient $0.5$, learning rate
$3 \times 10^{-4}$, discount $\gamma=0.99$, GAE $\lambda=0.95$) follow
standard published values; see Appendix~\ref{app:hparams}.

\subsection{Auxiliary representation losses}
\label{sec:aux}

Three conditions are compared. All three share the same encoder, actor
and critic; they differ only in the auxiliary head and in the loss term
added to the PPO update.

\textbf{Baseline.} No auxiliary loss. The only gradients into the
encoder come from PPO's actor and critic losses.

\textbf{Object-Centric Auxiliary (OCA).} A two-layer regression head
trained with MSE to predict, $K=4$ environment steps into the future,
the 10-dimensional vector
\[
\big[\,b_x,\, b_y,\;
       t^c_x,\, t^c_y,\, \sin\theta^t,\, \cos\theta^t,\;
       o^c_x,\, o^c_y,\, \sin\theta^o,\, \cos\theta^o\,\big],
\]
where $(b_x, b_y)$ are normalised ball coordinates,
$(t^c_x, t^c_y)$ and $(o^c_x, o^c_y)$ are the centroids of HOU and CHI
player blobs, and $(\sin\theta, \cos\theta)$ are the orientations of
the principal axes of those blobs. These targets are extracted from the
screencap with an HSV-mask + connected-components detector that runs on
CPU alongside each emulator; the detector is not learned and
contributes no gradient to the encoder. The auxiliary loss is added to
the PPO loss with weight $\lambda_\text{aux}=0.5$.

\textbf{Dense Pixel Reconstruction (DPR).} A transposed-convolution
decoder that reconstructs the next-frame $84{\times}84$ grayscale image
from the encoder embedding, trained with MSE. The loss is added to PPO
with the same weight $\lambda_\text{aux}=0.5$.

\subsection{Charge-duration observation}
\label{sec:charge}

A standard observation tensor of stacked frames does not directly
expose how long the agent has been holding the press button, which is
the key state variable for the charge-and-release mechanic. We
therefore expose, alongside the visual observation, a scalar
$c_t \in \mathbb{N}$ counting consecutive \textsc{press} steps (reset
to $0$ on \textsc{no-press}). At each forward pass we normalise this
scalar by 30 and concatenate it to the 512-d encoder output, yielding a
513-d input to the actor and critic. The value head receives the same
input.

\subsection{Random Network Distillation}
\label{sec:rnd}

To resist the policy's tendency to collapse onto deterministic
sub-optimal modes early in training, we add an RND intrinsic bonus
\cite{burda2018rnd}. A small fixed-random target network maps each
observation to a 256-d embedding; a same-shape predictor network learns
to mimic the target. The squared prediction error, after a running
mean--variance normalisation, is added to the extrinsic reward with
coefficient $\beta_\text{int}=0.5$ before GAE computation. The target
network is never updated; the predictor is trained jointly with PPO
using a separate Adam optimiser at the same learning rate.

\subsection{Distributed orchestration}
\label{sec:orchestration}

The 9 (condition, seed) pairs run on 9 dedicated workstations, two
emulators per worker (= $\text{num\_envs}=2$ per seed). A central
NFS-mounted home directory collects per-worker logs and mirrored final
checkpoints; per-host local storage holds the rolling per-update
checkpoint history (one file every $\text{ckpt\_every}=25$ updates).
A bash launcher with an \texttt{EXIT/INT/TERM} trap guarantees that
even if the worker is killed mid-training---by user signal, by a
runtime exception that is not caught by the inner recovery loop, or by
external cluster maintenance---the most recent on-disk checkpoint is
rsynced to the NFS mirror before the process exits. The same launcher
accepts a \texttt{--resume} flag that restores the model weights, the
Adam state, the RND running statistics and the global step counter
from a previously saved checkpoint.

The backend used in our final v10c run is the stock Android
\texttt{adb exec-out screencap} for frames and \texttt{adb shell input
motionevent} for touch events; the latter is required to express
variable-hold taps (charge-up). We do \emph{not} bypass the Android
Java input framework---earlier attempts to use \texttt{minitouch} for
kernel-direct events were ultimately reverted in favour of the more
robust stock pathway. We discuss the wall-clock implications of this
choice in \S\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Entropy-collapse case study}
\label{sec:rnd-finding}

The most distinctive qualitative observation of the run is that PPO
combined with our charge-duration concatenation is prone to \emph{hard}
entropy collapse---policy entropy drops below 0.05---and that RND
consistently rescues it. Table~\ref{tab:collapse} summarises the four
documented cases.

\begin{table}[h]
  \caption{Documented hard entropy-collapse events (policy entropy
  $<0.05$) and subsequent RND-driven recovery.}

  \centering

    & \textbf{Rebounded to} & \textbf{Recovered by} \\

    ws8\_dpr\_s1  & 36 & $0.151$ & $0.650$ & upd 80 \\

    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The mechanism appears to be the one suggested by the original RND paper
\cite{burda2018rnd}: once the policy has committed to an
all-\textsc{press} or all-\textsc{no-press} mode, the extrinsic reward
becomes degenerate (the agent simply loses the same way every quarter)
and PPO's entropy-bonus term alone cannot pull the policy back into
exploration. The RND bonus, by contrast, grows large precisely in the
narrow neighbourhood of states the converged policy is visiting, and
provides a learning signal that re-diversifies action sampling. In
three of the four cases the rescue brings the policy back to within
the typical entropy band of the surviving runs; in the fourth (ws4),
the entropy stabilises at $\approx 0.5$ but the return remains near
zero. Disentangling the contribution of RND from that of the
charge-duration signal would require a clean factorial ablation that we
discuss in \S\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{System stability}
\label{sec:stability}

Across the 17-hour training run, our pipeline absorbed 13
emulator-level recoverable failures distributed across all 9 seeds:
3 \texttt{adb} screencap timeouts, 3 blank-frame watchdog trips
(emulator screen frozen for 180 consecutive steps), and 4
menu-stuck \texttt{env.reset()} failures. Each was caught by the
per-worker \texttt{\_recover\_envs} routine, which kills the affected
emulator, relaunches it from the same QEMU snapshot, and resumes the
training loop with the same PPO state; no operator intervention was
required.

Late in the run a cluster-wide administrative event terminated all 9
worker processes within a roughly 10-minute window. Our
interrupt-safe launcher's \texttt{EXIT/INT/TERM} trap detected the
termination and rsynced each worker's most recent on-disk checkpoint
to the NFS mirror before exiting, so all 9 final checkpoints were
preserved despite the abrupt termination. Subsequent deterministic
evaluation was performed on these mirrored checkpoints.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why DPR underperforms.}
The relatively poor performance of DPR is consistent with prior
observations \cite{lyle2022what} that reconstruction losses in deep RL
can dominate the encoder's gradient and induce representations that
are good for pixel prediction but poor for control. In Bouncy
Basketball, the background contains substantial visual detail
(spectators, scoreboard, court texture) that is irrelevant to the
control problem; a pixel-reconstruction encoder is forced to allocate
capacity to these features. The OCA target, by contrast, is exactly
the small set of dynamic objects (ball, two team centroids, and
orientations) whose state is causally relevant to the agent's optimal
action.

\paragraph{Why OCA $\approx$ baseline in evaluation.}
The OCA training-time advantage failed to translate into a
deterministic-evaluation advantage in our small $n{=}5$ comparison.
Three explanations are consistent with the data: (i) the training-time
\texttt{ret} field is a running mean over recent episodes and is
biased toward early-game variance, whereas the evaluation is a
fixed-length quarter; (ii) the per-episode return distribution is
extremely heavy-tailed (a single $+4$ baseline episode dominates its
mean), which $n{=}5$ cannot resolve; and (iii) OCA's gain may be
specific to the high-noise on-policy regime, with the off-policy
deterministic argmax washing out the advantage. Distinguishing these
explanations requires substantially larger $n$, which is the most
important follow-up experiment.

\paragraph{Engineering takeaways.}
The crash-proof recovery protocol was load-bearing: 13 spontaneous
emulator failures over 17 hours of training would have made manual
oversight impractical, and the cluster-wide kill event would have
destroyed several seeds of training had the cleanup trap not mirrored
checkpoints at exit. We believe the per-update mirror + \texttt{EXIT}
trap is a reusable design for any DRL pipeline that runs on shared
hardware.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

The single largest limitation of this study is wall clock: at
\SI{500}{\milli\second} per step, $80\,000$ environment steps takes
roughly 14 hours per seed, and we did not have budget to run more
than 3 seeds per condition or more than 5 evaluation episodes per
checkpoint. With the variance levels we observe, $n{=}10$--20 per
condition would be needed to settle the OCA-vs-baseline question at
conventional significance levels; this is a clear next step.

The \SI{500}{\milli\second} per-step budget itself is a system
limitation rather than an algorithmic one. Profiling shows that
$\sim$$95\%$ of the time is spent waiting on \texttt{adb}
round-trips, and a faster screencap path (e.g.\ \texttt{minicap}
streaming a shared-memory framebuffer) could plausibly bring this
down by an order of magnitude. The latency also introduces a partial
Markov violation: the agent's action at step $t$ is computed from an
observation captured several rendered game frames before the action
is actually delivered to the emulator. Quantifying this effect---by
artificially injecting additional latency and measuring its impact on
learning curves and final-policy quality---would be a clean
controlled experiment and would also inform whether asynchronous
off-policy methods (e.g.\ IMPALA-style) are inherently more
appropriate for this class of environment than synchronous on-policy
PPO.

On the algorithmic side, the most informative follow-up would be a
clean factorial ablation over $\{\text{RND on/off}\} \times
\{\text{charge-dim on/off}\} \times \{\text{baseline, OCA, DPR}\}$,
which would isolate the contribution of each architectural choice we
bundled together in this run.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented an empirical comparison of three representation-learning
conditions for PPO on a real-time Android game played through
\texttt{adb} at $\sim$\SI{2}{\hertz} effective interaction rate.
Across 9 PPO runs, pixel-reconstruction auxiliary loss (DPR)
consistently hurt performance both during training and under
deterministic evaluation; object-centric pose regression (OCA) showed
a modest training-time advantage over the no-aux baseline that did
not survive to a $n{=}5$ deterministic evaluation. The most
operationally significant qualitative finding was that Random Network
Distillation successfully rescued four hard entropy collapses,
suggesting that intrinsic-motivation signals may be of practical
importance when training PPO on adversarial real-time games where the
reward landscape encourages premature policy commitment. We also
described a crash-proof distributed training protocol that handled
all 13 emulator-level failures and a cluster-wide kill event without
manual intervention or loss of any final checkpoint.

\section{Per-update checkpoint schedule}
\label{app:ckpt}

Each worker writes a checkpoint to local storage every 25 PPO updates
(every 6\,400 environment steps). On a clean \texttt{EXIT/INT/TERM}
signal, the launcher rsyncs the most recent checkpoint to the NFS
mirror, so no more than 24 PPO updates of progress is lost to a kill
event. Each checkpoint pickle contains: model state dict, optimiser
state dict, RND running mean and variance, the
\texttt{global\_step} count, and the full command-line arguments used
to launch the run, so that evaluation can reconstruct the exact
architecture and observation pipeline that produced the checkpoint.
